Fairness and Transparency Analysis of Hospital Readmission Prediction

Kostadin Devedzhiev
Centre for Human-Inspired AI, University of Cambridge
kgd26@cam.ac.uk
Abstract

We analyze fairness and transparency in a Random Forest classifier for 30-day hospital readmission using the Diabetes 130-Hospitals dataset (101,766 encounters). Adopting the "We Are All Equal" (WAE) worldview from Friedler et al., we evaluate group fairness via Demographic Parity and Equalized Odds using Fairlearn. For transparency, we employ SHAP and LIME, addressing the fidelity-interpretability tradeoff for different stakeholders. Our baseline exhibits significant disparities (Demographic Parity Difference = 0.248), which we reduce by 74.5% using an in-processing Exponentiated Gradient intervention, demonstrating fundamental fairness-accuracy tradeoffs. This work illustrates how responsible AI deployment requires explicit worldview selection, stakeholder-appropriate transparency, and honest engagement with fundamental tradeoffs.

1. Introduction

Machine learning in healthcare risks perpetuating bias against protected groups [Obermeyer et al., 2019]. Hospital readmission prediction exemplifies both individual harms (patients incorrectly flagged receive unnecessary interventions) and cumulative harms (systematic under-prediction for certain groups leads to resource misallocation). Such bias may be pre-existing (reflecting historical healthcare disparities), technical (introduced by model architecture), or emergent (arising from deployment context) [Friedman and Nissenbaum, 1996].

We address two critical dimensions of responsible AI: fairness and transparency (via feature attribution methods). Our contributions include: (1) fairness analysis situating disparities within theoretical frameworks, (2) transparency using SHAP and LIME with attention to stakeholder needs, and (3) in-processing intervention demonstrating inherent tradeoffs identified by impossibility theorems.

2. Background

2.1 Fairness: Worldviews, Metrics, and Impossibility

The fairness literature distinguishes two fundamental approaches [Dwork et al., 2012]. Group Fairness requires that protected group membership be irrelevant to classification outcomes—operationalized through metrics like Demographic Parity:

Individual Fairness requires similar treatment for similar individuals, formalized as a Lipschitz condition.

Friedler et al. [2021] articulate the worldviews underlying these approaches. The "What You See Is What You Get" (WYSIWYG) worldview assumes observed features faithfully represent underlying constructs—supporting individual fairness approaches. The "We Are All Equal" (WAE) worldview acknowledges that the mapping from Construct Space to Observed Space introduces structural bias aligned with group membership—justifying group-level corrections. We adopt the WAE perspective, recognizing that healthcare access disparities mean observed clinical features may systematically differ across racial groups independent of underlying health status.

Key Insight

Chouldechova [2017] and Kleinberg et al. [2017] proved fundamental impossibility results: when base rates differ across groups, simultaneously satisfying calibration, balance for the positive class, and balance for the negative class is mathematically impossible. Choosing fairness metrics involves value judgments about whose errors matter.

2.2 Transparency: From QII to Additive Feature Attribution

Transparency serves multiple goals: answering "how does the system work?", "why was I denied?", and "are decisions discriminatory?" [Datta et al., 2016]. The Quantitative Input Influence (QII) framework uses causal interventions to measure feature influence, using Shapley values from cooperative game theory to aggregate marginal influence across all possible feature subsets.

SHAP [Lundberg and Lee, 2017] provides a unified framework for "additive feature attribution methods" satisfying three axioms: (1) Local Accuracy—attributions sum to the prediction difference from baseline; (2) Missingness—absent features receive zero attribution; (3) Consistency—if a feature's marginal contribution increases, its attribution cannot decrease. These properties uniquely determine Shapley value allocation.

LIME [Ribeiro et al., 2016] attempts to address the fidelity-interpretability tradeoff: complex models are accurate but opaque, while interpretable surrogates are understandable but approximate. LIME samples locally, weights by proximity, and fits an interpretable model—navigating this tradeoff for individual predictions.

3. Methodology

3.1 Dataset and Problem Formulation

The Diabetes 130-Hospitals dataset [Clore and Strack, 2014] (101,766 encounters) maps from Construct Space (true readmission risk) through Observed Space (clinical features reflecting both health and healthcare access) to Decision Space (model predictions) [Friedler et al., 2021]. Key features include demographics (race, gender, age), clinical indicators (diagnoses, medications, lab procedures, hospital stay), and administrative data. The target is 30-day readmission (11.2% base rate).

3.4 Intervention: In-Processing

Fairness interventions are categorized as: pre-processing (modifying training data), in-processing (constraining learning), or post-processing (adjusting predictions). We apply Exponentiated Gradient [Agarwal et al., 2018]—an in-processing method that iteratively reweights samples to satisfy Equalized Odds constraints while minimizing accuracy loss.

The baseline exhibits DPD = 0.248 and EOD = 0.278, both substantially exceeding acceptable thresholds. From the WAE perspective, these disparities reflect how Observed Space features—shaped by differential healthcare access—distort the mapping from Construct Space. Caucasian patients are flagged at 2.8× the rate of Unknown patients, constituting disparate impact. African American patients face FPR of 0.348 versus 0.119 for Unknown patients, directly violating Equalized Odds. Gender disparities are less pronounced (DPD = 0.025, EOD = 0.055), suggesting race is the primary axis of unfairness.

The Exponentiated Gradient intervention substantially reduces disparities. DPD drops from 0.248 to 0.063—below the 0.1 threshold typically considered acceptable. The Demographic Parity Ratio improves from 0.355 to 0.881, approaching the ideal value of 1.0. This improvement comes at significant cost: accuracy drops from 64.5% to 53.0%. This 11.5 percentage point reduction represents the "price of fairness"—a direct manifestation of the impossibility theorems. When base rates differ across groups, achieving equal error rates reduces overall accuracy. The tradeoff is not a limitation of our method but a fundamental mathematical constraint.

5. Discussion

Our analysis operationalizes several key concepts:

  • Worldview Selection: By adopting WAE rather than WYSIWYG, we acknowledge that observed clinical features reflect structural healthcare disparities, justifying group-level fairness corrections.
  • Stakeholder-Specific Transparency: SHAP and LIME provide global importance for model developers and local explanations for clinicians and patients.
  • Impossibility Awareness: Our fairness-accuracy tradeoff directly demonstrates Chouldechova's findings, showing that choosing to optimize Equalized Odds prioritizes equal error rates over calibration—a value judgment requiring stakeholder input.

Limitations: (1) Explanations can be manipulated to conceal unfairness [Dimanov et al., 2020]; (2) SHAP reveals correlations, not causal pathways; (3) We optimize group fairness, potentially at individual fairness cost; (4) The dataset (1999–2008) may not reflect current clinical practices.

6. Conclusion

This work demonstrates systematic evaluation of fairness and transparency in healthcare ML. Key findings:

  1. Standard classifiers exhibit substantial fairness disparities (DPD = 0.248) invisible to aggregate accuracy metrics, validating the WAE worldview that observed features encode structural bias.
  2. SHAP and LIME provide complementary transparency serving different stakeholder needs, though explanations alone cannot guarantee fairness.
  3. In-processing interventions substantially reduce disparities (74.5% DPD improvement) at non-trivial accuracy cost (11.5 percentage points), reflecting the fundamental impossibility of simultaneously satisfying all fairness criteria.

Responsible AI deployment in healthcare requires explicit worldview engagement, stakeholder-appropriate transparency mechanisms, and honest acknowledgment that the question is not whether to accept tradeoffs, but which tradeoffs align with institutional values and patient welfare.
