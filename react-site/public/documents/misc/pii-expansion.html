ICO Know Your Data Workshop · 24–25 March 2026

PII Expansion

Scale anonymization outward from detected PII through coreference and constituency links.

Kostadin Devedzhiev · kgd26@cam.ac.uk · Workshop slide (PDF)

Motivation

Under Article 14 of the UK GDPR, organisations that collect personal data indirectly — such as through web scraping for AI training — must provide meaningful transparency to data subjects about how their data is processed. The ICO has identified that Foundation Model developers find it technically infeasible or disproportionately costly to identify personal data in web-scale training sets and notify individuals.

The ICO's March 2026 technical workshop (“Know your data: Designing AI transparency mechanisms”) frames this as a pipeline of challenge areas: identifying unique individuals in large-scale datasets, linking scattered records across web pages to those individuals, and notifying data subjects without exposing others' personal data.

Crucially, personal data under the UK GDPR extends well beyond direct identifiers. A name is a direct identifier, but “a serial tech entrepreneur with a decade of experience in sustainable innovation” or “38, previously co-founded SmartGrid Solutions” are also personal data that relates to and can help re-identify an individual. This distinction — between direct identifiers and the broader set of related personal data — is exactly the gap that standard PII detectors leave open.

The problem

Standard PII detectors find direct identifiers — names, emails, SSNs — but leave surrounding context intact. That context is often enough to re-identify someone. Consider:

[NAME] resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire. He is 29 years old.

The name is gone, but “resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire, 29 years old” narrows the identity to very few people. PII Expansion addresses this by masking outward from PII through the sentence structure.

How it works

1Detect

Presidio finds PII entities (names, emails, SSNs) — these become graph roots.

2Resolve

Coreference resolution links pronouns (he, she, his) back to the PII they refer to.

3Expand

A linking algorithm builds progressively larger text spans around each entity. Five algorithms are available.

The result is a graph where PII entities sit at the center and each ring outward represents one more level of contextual expansion. The scale slider controls how many rings to mask.

Example: constituency expansion

Take the sentence: Mr. Worthing resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire. Here is what each scale level masks:

1Direct PII only

Only the PII entity itself is masked. The name disappears, but the address remains fully intact.

Mr. Worthing resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire.
2PII + verb phrase

The next constituent is added — “resides” — linking the name to the action.

Mr. Worthing resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire.
3PII + address context

The prepositional phrase is now also masked, removing the address that could re-identify.

Mr. Worthing resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire.
4Full sentence

All remaining constituents are included. The entire sentence is redacted.

Mr. Worthing resides at The Manor House, Woolton, Hertfordshire.

Example: Shakespeare — The Merchant of Venice

PII expansion applies equally to literary and historical text. Consider this passage in the style of Shakespeare's The Merchant of Venice, dense with names, locations, and financial details:

1Direct PII only

Presidio detects three person entities — Antonio, Shylock, Bassanio — and the location “Venice.” Surrounding context (the bond amount, the flesh clause, Belmont) remains fully visible.

Signior Antonio, a merchant of Venice dwelling in the Rialto district, entered into a bond of three thousand ducats with Shylock of the Jewish quarter on the fourteenth of March. The bond was secured by a pound of Antonio's flesh, to be forfeited should he fail to repay within three months. His friend Bassanio, lately of Belmont, stood witness to the agreement.
2PII + immediate phrases

Sibling constituents are added: “Signior,” “a merchant of,” “dwelling in the Rialto district,” “of the Jewish quarter,” “His friend,” and “lately of Belmont.” The quasi-identifiers that characterise each person begin to disappear.

Signior Antonio, a merchant of Venice dwelling in the Rialto district, entered into a bond of three thousand ducats with Shylock of the Jewish quarter on the fourteenth of March. The bond was secured by a pound of Antonio's flesh, to be forfeited should he fail to repay within three months. His friend Bassanio, lately of Belmont, stood witness to the agreement.
3PII + verb phrases & clauses

Expansion now covers the main verb phrases — “entered into a bond of three thousand ducats,” “was secured by a pound of Antonio's flesh,” and “stood witness to the agreement.” The financial and contractual details are masked.

Signior Antonio, a merchant of Venice dwelling in the Rialto district, entered into a bond of three thousand ducats with Shylock of the Jewish quarter on the fourteenth of March. The bond was secured by a pound of Antonio's flesh, to be forfeited should he fail to repay within three months. His friend Bassanio, lately of Belmont, stood witness to the agreement.
4Full passage

All remaining constituents absorbed. The entire passage is redacted.

Signior Antonio, a merchant of Venice dwelling in the Rialto district, entered into a bond of three thousand ducats with Shylock of the Jewish quarter on the fourteenth of March. The bond was secured by a pound of Antonio's flesh, to be forfeited should he fail to repay within three months. His friend Bassanio, lately of Belmont, stood witness to the agreement.

Algorithm 1: Constituency Parse Tree Expansion

The constituency algorithm produces the finest-grained expansion by walking the parse tree bottom-to-top and adding sibling leaf constituents one at a time.

Algorithm 1 — ExpandFrom(node, p_s, p_e)
Input: Constituency parse tree node node for the sentence containing PII span [p_s, p_e) Output: Ordered list of expansion spans E = [(s_1, e_1), (s_2, e_2), ...] 1. Let C = [c_1, ..., c_k] be the children of node 2. if C is empty then return [] 3. Find index j such that c_j contains [p_s, p_e) 4. if no such j exists then return [] // Recurse into deeper tree levels first (bottom-up) 5. E_deep = ExpandFrom(c_j, p_s, p_e) // Collect leaf spans from sibling subtrees 6. R = [] (right siblings) 7. for i = j+1 to k do 8. R = R ++ LeafSpans(c_i) 9. L = [] (left siblings, reversed) 10. for i = j-1 downto 1 do 11. L = L ++ Reverse(LeafSpans(c_i)) // Initialize accumulator to PII-containing child's span 12. s_acc = start(c_j), e_acc = end(c_j) 13. E_level = [], r = 0, l = 0 // Interleave right-then-left, growing one leaf at a time 14. while r < |R| or l < |L| do 15. if r < |R| then 16. e_acc = max(e_acc, end(R[r])), r += 1 17. if (s_acc, e_acc) is new and span > [p_s, p_e) then 18. append (s_acc, e_acc) to E_level 19. if l < |L| then 20. s_acc = min(s_acc, start(L[l])), l += 1 21. if (s_acc, e_acc) is new and span > [p_s, p_e) then 22. append (s_acc, e_acc) to E_level 23. return E_deep ++ E_level

Worked example

Consider Mr. John Worthing resides at The Manor House with PII entity “John Worthing”. The constituency tree decomposes as:

S / \ NP VP / \ / \ NNP NNP VBZ PP Mr. John / \ Worthing IN NP at / \ DT NP The / \ NNP NNP Manor House

Starting from “John Worthing” inside the left NP, expansion proceeds:

Level 1
deeper recursion within NP — sibling leaf “Mr.” added
→ Mr. John Worthing
Level 2
S-level, right sibling VP, leaf 1 — “resides” added
→ Mr. John Worthing resides
Level 3
S-level, right sibling VP, leaf 2 — “at” added
→ Mr. John Worthing resides at
Level 4
S-level, right sibling VP, leaf 3 — “The” added
→ Mr. John Worthing resides at The
Level 5
S-level, right sibling VP, leaf 4 — “Manor” added
→ Mr. John Worthing resides at The Manor
Level 6
S-level, right sibling VP, leaf 5 — “House” added
→ Mr. John Worthing resides at The Manor House

This yields 6 expansion spans from a single PII entity, each adding exactly one terminal constituent — maximising granularity.

Example: coreference

Now consider a two-sentence passage:

Mr. Worthing resides at The Manor House. He was found in a handbag at Victoria Station.

Coreference resolution recognises that “He” refers to “Mr. Worthing”. Both are linked in the graph, so expanding from the name also pulls in the second sentence. Without this, scale 1 would mask only the name — leaving “He was found in a handbag at Victoria Station” as an obvious re-identification path.

Scoring

The Evaluate tab measures how thorough an anonymisation is. Given an original and anonymised text, it rebuilds the expansion graph and checks which entities still appear verbatim. The result is an achieved scale — how many hops from PII were fully covered.

Algorithm comparison

We implemented five linking algorithms and ran each on five test texts (employment, medical, financial, literary, long narrative). All share the same Presidio detection and coreference resolution stages — only the graph-building step differs.

The five algorithms

Constituency

phrase-level

Walks the benepar constituency parse tree. For each PII entity, accumulates sibling constituents outward, creating a chain of progressively larger phrase-level spans.

Dependency

word-level

BFS through spaCy's dependency graph from PII tokens. Each hop adds governors and dependents, producing progressively larger contiguous spans.

Co-occurrence

sentence-level

Links every entity to its containing sentence span, then chains adjacent sentences. Expansion radiates outward one sentence at a time from PII roots.

Semantic Roles

frame-level

Approximates SRL via dependency parse. Groups PII with co-arguments of the same verb frame (who/what/where), then expands to full sentences and neighbours.

Entity Clustering

entity-level

Groups PII by real-world entity using coreference + dependency tree walking + string matching. Links a person's name, SSN, email, and phone as one cluster, then expands to sentences.

Evaluation metrics

Three metrics capture different aspects of expansion quality. All are computed from the BFS expansion curve.

Max Scale (smax)

integer ≥ 1
s_max = eccentricity(PII roots) + 1

Definition: The largest scale value at which BFS discovers at least one new node.

Interpretation: Higher max scale means more discrete privacy levels. A max scale of 21 gives 21 distinct slider positions; a max scale of 4 gives only 4.

Distinct Coverage Levels

integer ≥ 1
|{ coverage(s) : s in [1, s_max] }|

Definition: The number of unique character-coverage values across all scales. Counts only steps where coverage actually changes.

Interpretation: The effective granularity. Constituency averages 17.6 distinct levels; co-occurrence averages only 2.2.

Full Coverage

percentage, 0–100%
coverage(s_max) = |masked_chars(s_max)| / |d|

Definition: The fraction of the document's characters masked when BFS has been exhausted.

Interpretation: Below 100% means some parts are unreachable from any PII root. Sentence-level algorithms achieve ~98.7%; constituency and dependency average ~91.8%.

Key findings

Constituency

Finest-grained control (avg 17.6 distinct levels, max scale 21.0). Best for precise privacy-utility tradeoffs. The deep parse tree produces many intermediate expansion steps.

Dependency

Strong middle ground (avg 4.0 levels, max scale 6.0). Walks the dep tree more gradually than constituency's phrase-level jumps, producing word-level expansion.

Co-occurrence

Jumps from ~14% to ~99% in one step. Simplest model: sentence-level on/off. Best coverage (98.7%) because sentence chaining reaches non-PII sentences.

Semantic Roles

Adds one meaningful intermediate level between PII and full sentences — the verb-frame arguments. Semantically motivated groupings.

Clustering

Groups PII by real-world entity using coreference, dependency walks, and string matching. Ensures a person's SSN, email, and name are always masked as a unit.

Recommendations

The right algorithm depends on the use case. No single approach dominates on every axis.

Maximum control
Constituency

When users need a fine-grained slider with many distinct privacy levels. Best for interactive tools where the operator tunes the tradeoff manually.

No extra models
Dependency

When you want to drop the benepar dependency. Uses only spaCy (already required for NER), giving 4 distinct levels and comparable coverage.

Full document coverage
Co-occurrence

When every sentence in the document must be reachable — even those without any PII anchor. Sentence chaining guarantees 98.7% avg coverage.

Semantic grouping
Semantic Roles

When the intermediate level should be semantically meaningful — “what happened involving this person” — rather than syntactic phrases.

Multi-person documents
Entity Clustering

When the text mentions multiple people with interleaved PII. Clustering ensures each person's attributes (name, SSN, email) are grouped and masked as a unit, preventing cross-entity leakage at scale 2.
